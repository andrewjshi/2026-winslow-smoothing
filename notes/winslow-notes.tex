\section{The 2016 formulation (brief recap)}

The Winslow equations in physical coordinates are
\begin{equation}
  g^{ij}\, \partial_i \partial_j x_k = 0, \qquad k = 1, \ldots, n,
\end{equation}
where $g^{ij}$ are the contravariant metric components of the (nonlinear)
mapping from the computational domain $C$ to the physical domain $D$.
The nonconservative form does not discretize cleanly in a CG
framework, so the 2016 paper introduces auxiliary variables
$\alpha_j = -\partial_i(g^{ij})$ and recasts the system in split form:
\begin{align}
  \partial_i(g^{ij}) + \alpha_j &= 0, \\
  \partial_i(g^{ij}\, \partial_j x_k) + \alpha_j\, \partial_j x_k &= 0,
\end{align}
with Dirichlet data $\xb = \xb_{\text{bnd}}$ on $\partial C$.
The point of this split form is to 1) mimic the what finite difference Winslow people are doing and 2) enable Picard iteration, which is the ``magic'' here.

To assess mesh quality we use the scaled Jacobian of
Fortunato--Persson~\cite{fortunato2016high},
\begin{equation}
  I \;=\; \frac{\min_{\xi \in \kappa} J(\xi)}{\max_{\xi \in \kappa} J(\xi)},
  \qquad I \leq 1,
  \label{eq:scaledJ}
\end{equation}
approximated at the Gauss quadrature points of each element~$\kappa$.
For a straight-sided simplex, $J$ is constant across $\kappa$ and
$I = 1$ exactly; $I \leq 0$ indicates a degenerate or inverted element.

any simplex with straight edges has $I = 1$ regardless of aspect
ratio, so it cannot ``see'' the sliver patch in the reference. To
capture straight-sided shape distortion we also report the Liu--Joe
inverse mean-ratio shape distortion

\begin{equation}
  \eta \;=\; \frac{\|\J\|_F^2}{d \, |\det \J|^{2/d}},
  \qquad \eta \in [1, \infty),
  \label{eq:eta}
\end{equation}

as the element degenerates. The results are summarized in

\section{$W$ modification to achieve reference element shape}
\label{sec:W-mod}

The 2016 formulation produces a physical mesh whose element shapes are
inherited from the reference mesh: the contravariant metric $g^{ij}$
in the split-form system of Section~0 is built from the reference
element geometry, so a skewed reference produces skewed physical
elements. The $W$-modification below decouples the target shape from
the reference element shape.
\textbf{This is exactly the same formulation Persson gave me from his
Gemini conversation
(\href{run:persson_gemini_winslow.pdf}{persson\_gemini\_winslow.pdf}).}

\subsection{Target metric}

For each reference element $K \in \mathcal{T}_h$, let $W_K \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$
denote the Jacobian of an affine map from an ideal element (e.g.\ a
unit equilateral simplex or unit square) to the reference element
geometry. The total map from the ideal element to the physical
element then has Jacobian $J_{\mathrm{tot}} = J W_K^{-1}$, where
$J = \partial x/\partial \xi$ is the reference-to-physical Jacobian.

The covariant and contravariant metrics of the total map are
\begin{equation}
  \tilde g_{ij} = (J_{\mathrm{tot}}^T J_{\mathrm{tot}})_{ij}
                = (W_K^{-T} g W_K^{-1})_{ij},
  \qquad
  \tilde g^{ij} = (W_K g^{-1} W_K^T)_{ij}
                = W_{K,ik}\, g^{kl}\, W_{K,jl}.
  \label{eq:tildeg}
\end{equation}
With $W_K = I$ for all $K$, $\tilde g^{ij} = g^{ij}$ and the 2016
formulation is recovered.

\subsection{Modified split form}

We replace $g^{ij}$ by $\tilde g^{ij}$ in the split-form system of
Section~0, introducing a modified auxiliary variable
$\tilde\alpha_j = -\partial_i \tilde g^{ij}$:
\begin{align}
  \partial_i(\tilde g^{ij}) + \tilde\alpha_j &= 0, \label{eq:Wsplit-alpha}\\
  \partial_i(\tilde g^{ij}\, \partial_j x_k)
    + \tilde\alpha_j\, \partial_j x_k &= 0,
    \label{eq:Wsplit-x}
\end{align}
with Dirichlet data $x = x_{\mathrm{bnd}}$ on $\partial C$. The
equivalence with the nonconservative form
$\tilde g^{ij}\partial_i\partial_j x_k = 0$ follows from
\eqref{eq:Wsplit-alpha} by the same product-rule argument as in the
unmodified case.

\paragraph{Heuristic nature of the extension.}\label{sec:W-heuristic}
The classical Winslow system $g^{ij}\partial_i\partial_j x_k = 0$ is
the reformulation, via change of variables, of $\Delta_x \xi_k = 0$---the
Euler--Lagrange equation of the Dirichlet energy $\int_D |\nabla_x \xi|^2\,dx$
for the inverse map. The substitution $g \mapsto \tilde g$ in
\eqref{eq:Wsplit-alpha}--\eqref{eq:Wsplit-x} is not, to the authors'
knowledge, the Euler--Lagrange equation of any natural energy
functional in $x$. In particular, for piecewise-constant $W_K$ the
map $\xi \mapsto W_K \xi$ is a per-element linear reparameterization
of the reference coordinates, and harmonic maps are invariant under
such reparameterizations, so the natural variational interpretation
collapses to the unmodified Winslow system. We therefore regard
\eqref{eq:Wsplit-alpha}--\eqref{eq:Wsplit-x} as a heuristic extension
of the 2016 numerical scheme, parameterized so as to retain its
Picard structure, rather than as the discretization of a derived PDE.
Consequences: no Rad\'o--Kneser--Choquet analog applies, and
convergence of the resulting Picard iteration is assessed empirically.

\subsection{Continuity of $W$ and the $\tilde\alpha$ projection}
\label{sec:W-continuity}

The original Fortunato--Persson formulation already solves one
projection problem: the auxiliary variable $\alpha_j$ is defined,
through the weak form of~$\partial_i g^{ij} + \alpha_j = 0$, as the
$L^2$ projection of $-\partial_i g^{ij}$ onto the continuous CG
space $V_h^p$. Operationally this is the linear solve
$\alpha \leftarrow M \backslash b$, where $M$ is the $V_h^p$ mass
matrix and $b$ is assembled by quadrature from $g^{ij}$ at Gauss
points. The solve is what makes the scheme work on unstructured
meshes. Because $x_h \in V_h^p$ is $C^0$ but not $C^1$, the gradient
$\partial x / \partial \xi$ jumps across every internal edge, so
$g^{ij}$ is discontinuous there, and a strong derivative of $g^{ij}$
would produce Dirac-style spikes supported on the interfaces. By
omitting internal face fluxes in the weak form, the projection
smears those spikes into a single continuous $\alpha \in V_h^p$,
bypassing the DG machinery of numerical fluxes entirely. This is
the computational magic of the 2016 paper.

In the modified system~\eqref{eq:Wsplit-alpha}--\eqref{eq:Wsplit-x}
this projection is reused without modification. $\tilde\alpha_j$ is
the $V_h^p$ projection of $-\partial_i \tilde g^{ij}$, computed with
the \emph{same} mass matrix $M$ and the same backslash solve; only
the right-hand side is altered to use
$\tilde g^{ij} = W_K g^{ij} W_K^T$ instead of $g^{ij}$. No new
projection infrastructure is introduced at this stage, and one can
formally proceed without any further thought about the role of
$W_K$.

The genuinely new object is $W_K$. It is the Jacobian of the affine
map from the ideal equilateral triangle to the (straight-sided)
reference triangle $K$---a map that sends the three corners of the
ideal element to the three corners of $K$. Because the map is
affine, its Jacobian $W_K$ is a \emph{single} $2 \times 2$ matrix,
constant throughout the interior of $K$, at every polynomial order
$p$; the polynomial order $p$ controls how the \emph{solution} $x_h$
is represented, not the geometry of $K$, and the straight-sided
reference keeps $W_K$ element-constant regardless of $p$. The
subscript $K$ records the essential point that $W_K$ is one matrix
per element and jumps discontinuously across interfaces, since
adjacent reference triangles are mapped from the ideal by different
affine transformations.

At a glance it is tempting to conclude that these new jumps are
absorbed for free by the $\tilde\alpha$-projection, just as the old
jumps of $g^{ij}$ were. The projection is indeed well-defined, but
a subtler point intervenes. The jumps of $g^{ij}$ at an interface
are controlled by the jumps of $\partial x_h / \partial \xi$ there,
and these shrink to zero as the Picard iteration converges, since
the fixed point $x_h$ is as smooth as the discrete space allows.
FP's robustness relies on this self-limiting behavior: the
coefficient roughness that the iteration has to cope with decays
alongside the iteration itself. The jumps of $W_K$ are structurally
different. They are set once and for all by the initial reference
mesh, are external to the iterate, and \emph{do not} decay with
Picard. If the reference mesh contains neighbors of very different
shape, the $W_K$ jumps can be substantial, and we have observed
empirically that the resulting persistent coefficient roughness
destabilizes the iteration.

The remedy suggested in the Persson/Gemini exchange is to elevate
$W$ to $V_h^p$ \emph{before} forming $\tilde g^{ij}$. At each CG
node $x_n$, assemble a nodal target $W_h(x_n)$ as a weighted average
of the $W_K$ values from the surrounding elements,
\begin{equation}
  W_h(\mathbf{x}_n)
    = \frac{\sum_{K \ni \mathbf{x}_n} |K|\, W_K}
           {\sum_{K \ni \mathbf{x}_n} |K|},
  \label{eq:Wnodal}
\end{equation}
then interpolate via the standard shape functions. The resulting
$W_h \in V_h^p$ is $C^0$, so $\tilde g^{ij} = W_h g^{ij} W_h^T$
inherits \emph{only} the jumps already present in $g^{ij}$, which
the $\tilde\alpha$-projection handles as before. The averaging
itself is cheap: it is a lumped-mass $L^2$ projection onto $V_h^p$,
equivalent to area-weighted nodal averaging. A full (non-lumped)
$L^2$ projection would require an additional global mass-matrix
solve but is not necessary, since the Winslow-like operator is
relatively insensitive to moderate blur in the target metric.

\paragraph{Running the same modification on the skewed reference
(\texttt{w2\_skewed\_W.m}).}
Applying the same $W$-modified solver to the \texttt{w2\_skewed}
setup of Section~0.1---the sliver-cluster reference---produces
qualitatively different behavior: the Picard iteration does not
converge. After an initial residual of $\approx 5$, successive
iterates oscillate in the range $0.4$--$5$ for roughly thirty
steps, then amplify to residuals of $20$--$90$, and finally hit
the $100$-iteration cap with no sign of settling. The unmodified
solver, by contrast, converges cleanly on the same setup in
$14$ iterations (Figure~\ref{fig:w2_skewed}). The divergence is
not an implementation artefact; \texttt{w\_regression.m} continues
to pass.

This is the failure mode anticipated qualitatively in
Section~\ref{sec:W-continuity}, where we noted that the jumps of
$W_K$ (or, more generally, the gradient of $W_h$) are
\emph{external} to the iterate and do not decay with Picard,
unlike the jumps of $g^{ij}$ which shrink as $\partial x_h /
\partial \xi$ smooths. The product-rule expansion of
$\tilde\alpha_j = -\partial_i \tilde g^{ij}$ makes this concrete.
With $\tilde g^{ij} = W_h^{ik} g^{kl} W_h^{jl}$,
\begin{equation}
  \tilde\alpha_j
  \;=\; -\,W_h^{ik} W_h^{jl}\,\partial_i g^{kl}
       \;-\; 2\,(\partial_i W_h) \cdot g \cdot W_h.
  \label{eq:tildealpha-split}
\end{equation}
The first term is the original $\alpha$ conjugated by $W_h$.
Like the unmodified $\alpha$, it is driven by jumps in
$\partial x / \partial \xi$, which shrink as $x_h$ relaxes toward
its fixed point---the iteration's self-limiting smoothing. The
second term is \emph{entirely new} and is independent of the
current iterate: $W_h$ is fixed once by the reference geometry,
so $\partial_i W_h$ never decays with Picard. When the reference
mesh is uniform, $W_h$ is a constant field, $\partial_i W_h = 0$,
and this new term vanishes---which is why \texttt{w2\_W.m}
converges and only exhibits a mild fixed-point inconsistency (the
two inverted interior elements) rather than an iteration-level
failure. When the reference contains a sliver patch, $W_h$ varies
sharply across a handful of elements and $\partial_i W_h$ is
locally large; the new term contributes a persistent,
iteration-independent forcing to $\tilde\alpha$ that neither the
$V_h^p$ projection nor the Picard loop can absorb.

Functionally, $\tilde\alpha_j$ is the advection coefficient in the
$x$-equation
$\partial_i(\tilde g^{ij}\partial_j x_k)
 + \tilde\alpha_j\,\partial_j x_k = 0$.
Large, persistent $\tilde\alpha$ pushes the operator into the
advection-dominated regime that standard continuous Galerkin
treats badly without stabilization (SUPG, upwinding, etc.), and in
the Picard loop manifests as an increasingly-noisy update that
eventually exceeds the iteration's natural contraction rate. The
failure in \texttt{w2\_skewed\_W.m} is therefore not a quirk of
our particular discretization choice but a consequence of the
$W$-modification itself once $\partial W_h \neq 0$.

This observation is important on its own, independent of any
specific cure, because it explains why the scheme as literally
written (following the Persson/Gemini exchange) cannot simply be
``used''. The iteration's self-limiting coefficient smoothing,
which is what makes the 2016 formulation so robust, is broken by
the $W$-modification in exactly the regime the modification is
supposed to help. Before attempting to stabilize the iteration,
it is worth understanding this.

\subsection{Summary}
\label{sec:W-summary}

The $W$-modification of Section~\ref{sec:W-mod}, taken literally as
the product-rule substitution $g^{ij} \mapsto W_h g^{ij} W_h^T$ in
the 2016 split-form iteration, does not inherit the empirical
robustness of the original formulation. Two reasons:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \emph{Picard's self-limiting smoothing is broken.} The
    product-rule expansion of $\tilde\alpha_j = -\partial_i
    \tilde g^{ij}$ introduces a new term $-2\,(\partial_i W_h)
    \cdot g \cdot W_h$ that is independent of the current iterate.
    It persists as a smooth forcing in $\tilde\alpha$ that the
    $L^2$ projection cannot absorb and that Picard cannot relax,
    because it is not driven by $\partial x_h / \partial \xi$.
    Wherever $W_h$ varies in space---which is exactly where the
    modification is supposed to do useful work---this term makes
    the $x$-equation advection-dominated and Picard
    non-contractive. Empirically, a single moved interior vertex
    in the \texttt{w2\_skewed} reference is enough to send the
    iteration into oscillatory divergence. Smoothing $W_h$ via
    lumped or consistent $L^2$ projection onto $V_h^p$ reduces the
    magnitude of $\partial W_h$ locally but does not eliminate
    it---projection trades interface spikes for interior gradients
    of comparable integrated magnitude. The only way to set
    $\partial W_h = 0$ is to take $W_h$ globally constant, which
    defeats the purpose of the modification.

  \item \emph{There is no energy whose Euler--Lagrange equation is
    the modified split form.} A gradient of a scalar functional
    in~$x$ cannot have a non-zero iterate-independent piece, and
    the $\partial W_h$ term is exactly that. This rules out any
    natural variational interpretation in~$x$: there is no
    Lyapunov function to certify convergence, no minimum principle
    to guarantee non-inversion at the fixed point, and no standard
    machinery (line search, backtracking, Newton with globalisation)
    to stabilize the iteration. We are left with a heuristic
    fixed-point scheme without convergence theory.
\end{enumerate}

The two observations are not independent: it is because the scheme
is not a gradient that the $\partial W_h$ term can appear at all.
What makes the 2016 formulation's Picard so robust is precisely the
self-consistency condition $\alpha_j = -\partial_i g^{ij}$, which
makes $\alpha$ a computed consequence of the iterate; the
$W$-modification breaks this by forcing $\tilde\alpha$ to absorb an
external contribution. No amount of adjusting the $W_h$ projection
or the lumping recovers this structure. The modification is
attractive on paper but the iteration machinery it was slotted into
does not tolerate it.

\section{Reference-mesh preprocessing via Tutte's embedding}
\label{sec:tutte}

Motivated by the negative findings of Section~\ref{sec:W-mod}, we
take a different approach. Rather than modifying the Fortunato--Persson
iteration to correct for a poor reference mesh, we commit to using
the 2016 scheme \emph{as a black box} and instead construct a
well-shaped reference mesh with the same topology.

The whole chain is: given a poorly shaped, possibly tangled high-order 2D mesh, we
produce a straight-sided reference mesh with the \emph{same
connectivity} whose element shapes are as close to ideal as the
topology permits, and then we feed that reference into the
2016 framework with the original mesh's boundary as
Dirichlet data.

The preprocessing chain has four steps, each taken up in a
subsection below:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Extract the topology} (Section~\ref{sec:tutte-topology}).
    Discard the positions of the input mesh and keep only its
    combinatorial structure: the vertex list, the triangle list,
    and the identification of boundary vertices.
  \item \textbf{Tutte's barycentric embedding}
    (Section~\ref{sec:tutte-embedding}). Map the boundary vertices
    to a convex polygon (e.g.\ the unit square), then solve
    Tutte's linear system for the interior vertex positions. The
    output is a straight-sided planar mesh with the input topology,
    guaranteed to have no inverted or degenerate elements.
  \item \textbf{Mesh optimization}
    (Section~\ref{sec:tutte-shape}). \emph{The key observation
    is that this step acts on a straight-sided, untangled mesh.}
    The Tutte output of Step~2 is guaranteed to have straight
    edges and positive element Jacobians, so standard mesh-quality
    optimization---which is notoriously ill-posed on tangled or
    curved input---can be applied cleanly. We minimize a shape
    functional (e.g.\ the Liu--Joe shape distortion
    $\eta$~\eqref{eq:eta}) while keeping the topology fixed and
    the vertex positions valid. This turns the ``valid but
    possibly ugly'' Tutte output into a ``valid and well-shaped''
    straight-sided reference candidate.
  \item \textbf{Promotion to high order}
    (Section~\ref{sec:tutte-highorder}). Apply \texttt{nodealloc}
    to allocate the $V_h^p$ nodes on the straight edges of the
    optimized mesh, producing a valid high-order reference mesh
    that matches the input's polynomial degree.
\end{enumerate}

The resulting reference mesh is then used as the input to
\texttt{elliptic\_smoothing.m} with the original (possibly tangled)
mesh's boundary node positions as Dirichlet data. The 2016 scheme
proceeds unmodified from that point.

\subsection{Extracting the topology}
\label{sec:tutte-topology}

Given the input high-order mesh we discard its geometric data---both
the linear vertex positions and the high-order node
positions---and retain only its combinatorial structure: the list of
triangles (triples of linear-vertex indices) and the identification
of which edges lie on the exterior boundary. This is enough to run
Tutte's algorithm, which operates on the abstract planar graph of
the mesh. Whether the input positions were tangled, inverted, badly
shaped, or perfectly valid is irrelevant at this stage: the data
Tutte sees is the same list of vertices and the same list of
triangle connectivities.

Two structural assumptions on the input are required. First, the
mesh must be topologically a disk---simply connected with a single
exterior boundary. Second, its linear-vertex graph must be
$3$-connected. Both are satisfied by any standard triangulation of a
simply connected planar domain with more than three vertices. Given
these, the boundary forms a single cyclic loop of linear vertices,
which we extract by walking the edges whose faces have no neighbor
in the triangle-to-triangle connectivity. All remaining vertices are
classified as interior.

\subsection{Tutte's barycentric embedding}
\label{sec:tutte-embedding}

\textbf{Tutte's theorem}~(1963): if the boundary vertices of a
$3$-connected planar graph are pinned to a convex polygon and each
interior vertex is placed at the centroid of its graph neighbors,
then the resulting straight-line drawing is a valid planar
embedding---no triangle is inverted or degenerate. The theorem is
striking in that it produces a globally valid mesh from purely local
barycentric conditions, with no optimization loop, line search, or
sign-of-Jacobian tracking.

Given a chosen convex target polygon $P$ (the computational domain
we want our reference mesh to live in), the $N$ boundary vertices
extracted in Section~\ref{sec:tutte-topology} are distributed
monotonically around~$\partial P$ by \textbf{proportional arc
length}: if two consecutive boundary vertices are at arc-length
distance $\ell$ on the physical boundary (out of a total physical
perimeter $L$), they are placed at arc-length distance
$|\partial P| \cdot \ell / L$ on the target boundary. In other
words, boundary vertex spacing on $\partial P$ mirrors the
spacing on the physical boundary in proportion. This preserves
the \emph{local density} of boundary vertices (vertices per
unit arc length) under the map: dense regions on the physical
stay dense on the target, sparse regions stay sparse. What is
\emph{not} preserved is the 2D area backing each boundary
segment; we return to the consequences of that mismatch when
we discuss clumping in the starfish example
(Section~\ref{sec:tutte-ex-starfish}).

Each interior vertex
$\mathbf{x}_i$ is then required to satisfy
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{x}_i \;=\; \frac{1}{\deg(i)} \sum_{j \sim i} \mathbf{x}_j,
  \qquad i \in \text{interior},
  \label{eq:tutte-bary}
\end{equation}
where the sum is over the linear-vertex neighbors $j$ of $i$ and
$\deg(i)$ is the valence. Collecting one such equation per interior
vertex and moving the known boundary contributions to the
right-hand side gives the sparse linear system
\begin{equation}
  L_{\mathrm{ii}}\, \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{int}}
    \;=\; -\, L_{\mathrm{ib}}\, \mathbf{x}_{\mathrm{bnd}},
  \label{eq:tutte-linear}
\end{equation}
where $L = D - A$ is the graph Laplacian of the linear-vertex
connectivity ($D$ diagonal of valences, $A$ the adjacency matrix),
$L_{\mathrm{ii}}$ is its restriction to interior--interior entries,
and $L_{\mathrm{ib}}$ its restriction to interior--boundary entries.
The system is symmetric positive definite, and each of the two
spatial components of $\mathbf{x}$ is solved independently with a
single sparse direct call.

A few practical notes:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Boundary parameterization.} We use
    proportional-arc-length placement as described above. Any
    monotone bijection from the input boundary loop
    onto $\partial P$ would produce a valid Tutte embedding;
    the proportional-arc choice is what keeps the map's local
    information content tied to the physical---it gives the
    clumping phenomenon a clean diagnostic interpretation
    instead of tangling it with an arbitrary boundary-placement
    choice. Uniform-arc (every boundary vertex equally spaced
    on $\partial P$, regardless of physical) is a simpler
    alternative but loses that correspondence.
  \item Tutte's output is uniquely determined by the topology and
    the boundary map. Distinct topologies produce distinct
    embeddings; distinct boundary parameterisations of the same
    topology produce distinct embeddings.
  \item The output is valid in the sense that every triangle has
    positive area, but it is not optimized for element shape.
    Vertices with high or irregular valence, or whose graph
    neighborhood is highly asymmetric, typically produce
    short-edge or long-edge triangles. Improving those shapes is
    the job of Section~\ref{sec:tutte-shape}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Shape optimization of the straight-sided mesh}
\label{sec:tutte-shape}

The Tutte output of Section~\ref{sec:tutte-embedding} is a valid
straight-sided triangulation of the target polygon~$P$: boundary
vertices are pinned to $\partial P$, interior vertices sit at
Laplacian positions, every element has positive area. We now
relocate the interior vertices to improve element shapes. Two
features of this step make it tractable: the input is untangled
(every element has positive area) and straight-sided (every Jacobian
$J_K$ is a single $2 \times 2$ matrix per element). Both of these
fail for the original input mesh---which is why this optimization is
deferred to after Tutte.

On each element $K$, the Liu--Joe shape distortion of
Equation~\eqref{eq:eta} reduces to
\begin{equation}
  \eta_K \;=\; \frac{\|J_K\|_F^2}{d\, |\det J_K|^{2/d}},
  \qquad \eta_K \ge 1,
\end{equation}
where $J_K$ is the Jacobian of the affine map from the ideal
(equilateral) simplex to $K$. For the present linear problem
$J_K$ is an explicit, closed-form function of the three corner
positions of $K$.

We aggregate these per-element values into a single scalar
objective over the interior-vertex positions $\mathbf{x}$:
\begin{equation}
  F(\mathbf{x}) \;=\; \sum_{K \in \mathcal{T}_h} \eta_K(\mathbf{x})^2.
  \label{eq:tutte-F}
\end{equation}
Squaring, rather than taking the plain sum, has two practical
effects. First, it penalizes outlier elements quadratically, so a
single near-degenerate element cannot hide inside a large overall
sum of moderate values. Second, and more importantly, $\eta_K$
diverges as $|\det J_K| \to 0$; the squared objective inherits this
divergence and acts as an effective infinite barrier against
element inversion. Because the Tutte output in
Section~\ref{sec:tutte-embedding} gives a strictly positive Jacobian
at every element, we start inside this barrier, and any descent
direction that respects $F$ stays inside it.

The problem is a standard unconstrained nonlinear optimization over
$\mathbb{R}^{2 n_{\mathrm{int}}}$ (two coordinates per interior
vertex, boundary vertices held fixed). Gradients are
analytic---$J_K$ is affine in the three corner positions, so
$\eta_K$ is a rational function of the interior-vertex coordinates
with explicit derivatives---and standard quasi-Newton solvers
(L-BFGS, trust-region Newton, BFGS with Armijo line search) converge
without special handling. The Tutte output is the initial guess.

This is the straight-sided specialization of the curved-element
mesh optimization problem of
Shi--Persson~\cite{shi2023local}, whose Equation~(4) generalizes
$\eta_K$ to high-order elements by integrating a pointwise
distortion over each curved element. In the present pipeline we do
not need that generalization because the mesh entering the
optimization step is straight-sided by construction.

\subsection{Promotion to high-order reference}
\label{sec:tutte-highorder}

The output of Section~\ref{sec:tutte-shape} is a straight-sided
linear triangulation of the target polygon~$P$: fixed topology
inherited from the input, boundary vertices pinned, interior
vertices placed at a minimizer of the discrete shape objective.
Nothing about the high-order structure has been touched yet.

Promoting to high order is trivial: run
\texttt{nodealloc(msh, porder)} with the same polynomial order as
the input mesh. The routine allocates high-order DG nodes by
interpolating the shape functions on each straight-sided element
from its three linear corner positions. Because the linear
triangulation is straight-sided, there is no ambiguity: high-order
nodes sit exactly where the element's barycentric interpolation
places them, i.e., along straight edges and on a regular pattern
in the interior of each element. The resulting high-order mesh
inherits the element quality of the linear mesh from
Section~\ref{sec:tutte-shape}: every $J_K$ of the reference element
is still the same $2 \times 2$ affine Jacobian, and the Liu--Joe
distortion $\eta_K$ at each element is the same value it had at the
end of the shape optimization.

With this reference mesh in hand, we hand it to
\texttt{elliptic\_smoothing.m}, the original 2016
Fortunato--Persson solver, with the \emph{original} input mesh's
boundary node positions as Dirichlet data. No further modification
is needed---the smoother runs in exactly the regime it was designed
for: a well-shaped straight-sided reference with a prescribed
(possibly curved) physical boundary.

\paragraph{Clumping on the Tutte disk as a diagnostic.}
We have now seen two different physical geometries produce
structured sliver clumps on the Tutte disk. The stadium
(Section~\ref{sec:tutte-ex-stadium},
Fig.~\ref{fig:stadium-pipeline}\subref{fig:stadium-tutte})
produced two clumps, one per semicircular end-cap on the
physical. The starfish
(Fig.~\ref{fig:star-pipeline}\subref{fig:star-tutte}) produces
five clumps, one per arm. In both cases the clumps are
\emph{localized} to specific arcs of the disk and the
top/bottom/body portions of the disk are comparatively
clump-free. A unifying interpretation:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Under proportional-arc Tutte
    (Section~\ref{sec:tutte-embedding}), the boundary-vertex
    density on the reference arc matches that on the
    corresponding physical segment.
  \item The \emph{interior} triangles attached to that
    boundary segment are placed by Tutte
    (Section~\ref{sec:tutte-embedding}). If the physical
    region backed by that boundary segment has small area per
    boundary vertex (dense boundary sampling), Tutte ends up
    placing the adjacent interior vertices \emph{close} to the
    corresponding short disk arc. The resulting triangle is
    thin in both the tangential direction (its two boundary
    vertices are close together on the arc) and the radial
    direction (the interior vertex is pulled toward the arc)---a
    boundary-hugging sliver. Many such triangles side-by-side
    form the clump.
  \item \emph{What makes boundary sampling locally dense
    varies by domain}. For the stadium it is
    \emph{boundary curvature}: Delaunay resolves the curved
    caps with many small edges while the straight sides use
    few large edges. For the starfish it is
    \emph{narrow 2D shape}: each arm has moderate perimeter
    share but only $\sim 1/5$ of the area, so all of its
    triangles are boundary-adjacent.
  \item The resulting diagnostic is the same: each clump on
    the disk points at a specific physical region where
    boundary sampling is locally dense relative to interior
    area. Equivalently, for a convex target~$P$, a physical
    region gets an angular wedge of~$P$ whose opening is set
    by its perimeter share, not its area share; when the two
    shares disagree (elongated protrusions, tightly-sampled
    curves), the triangles inside are compressed tangentially
    into slivers on the disk. The mechanism causing the
    mismatch differs by domain; the pipeline handles both the
    same way.
  \item What Winslow does about it: it maps each slivered
    wedge back to its narrow physical region, compressing
    triangles tangentially and stretching them radially, which
    undoes the Tutte-side distortion. For smooth physical
    boundaries this graceful transport recovers moderate
    aspect ratios---see
    Fig.~\ref{fig:star-pipeline}(\subref{fig:star-winslow})
    for the starfish and Fig.~\ref{fig:stadium-pipeline}(e)
    for the stadium. It is not guaranteed in general; for
    non-smooth physical boundaries the inverse map can fail
    to fully unwind the compression, as we will see in
    Example~\ref{sec:tutte-ex-lshape}.
\end{itemize}

The two Winslow outputs differ substantially in favor of the
shape-opt path (Table~\ref{tab:starfish-comparison}). On the
$218$-element starfish mesh, shape-opt cuts mean $\eta^2$ by

\paragraph{Aside: what shape optimization actually optimizes.}
Shape-opt minimizes $\sum_K \eta_K^2$, which measures
\emph{shape} quality, not element \emph{size}. In the starfish

Table~\ref{tab:starfish-comparison}). Visual size-uniformity and
shape quality are different objectives.

\subsubsection{Example 5: non-convex L-shape, two target shapes}

introduces a reflex corner at $(0.5,0.5)$ and six boundary corners
total. The input is a constrained Delaunay of the L with
$30$~boundary samples and $41$~interior points
(panel~(a) of Figures~\ref{fig:l-square-pipeline}
and~\ref{fig:l-circle-pipeline}).

Because the L-shape is non-convex, the Tutte target~$P$ must be a
convex polygon containing it topologically---some convex region we
will use as the computational domain before handing off to
Fortunato--Persson. We run the pipeline with two natural choices
of~$P$: the unit square and the unit disk.

\paragraph{Unit-square target.}
Figure~\ref{fig:l-square-pipeline} shows the four stages. Tutte
with $P = [0,1]^2$ produces a valid straight-sided reference
(\subref{fig:l-sq-tutte}, $\max \eta = 7.35$) on which shape
optimization does real work, pulling $\max \eta$ down to $4.15$
over $O(10^2)$ BFGS iterations
(\subref{fig:l-sq-opt}). Feeding this reference to
\texttt{elliptic\_smoothing.m} with the L-shape boundary as
Dirichlet data
(\subref{fig:l-sq-winslow}) produces a mesh of the physical
L-shape, but with $\min I = -0.019$ (one element slightly
inverted, shown in red) and $\max \eta = 10.84$. The failure is
geometric. For a polygonal target, Tutte has to pin some
boundary vertices to the target's corners, otherwise the
boundary it embeds onto is a corner-chopped polygon inscribed in
the target rather than the target itself. The square-target code
picks four boundary vertices by uniform index spacing around the
boundary loop and pins them to the four square corners,
independent of which vertices are L corners. For this mesh none
of the L's six corners sit at the four chosen indices, so all six
end up on the interior of straight square edges (red markers in
\subref{fig:l-sq-tutte}). When Winslow maps the reference back to
the L-shape, each of those six vertices has to develop a sharp
(or reflex) corner out of a straight reference neighborhood, and
the worst case produces the inverted element in
\subref{fig:l-sq-winslow}.

\paragraph{Aside: what happens if we let boundary nodes slide?}
The pipeline as described above keeps boundary vertices pinned at
two places: during shape-opt (boundary vertices stay at the
Tutte-assigned disk positions) and during Winslow (boundary
vertices stay at the physical L-shape positions, connected to the
reference boundary by index). A natural question is whether
\emph{sliding} boundary vertices along $\partial P$ or
$\partial \Omega$---pinning only the L's six corners, letting
every other boundary vertex move along its assigned edge of the
disk or the L---could produce a better physical mesh. Crossing
the two possibilities at each stage gives four configurations:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{A. Pinned shape-opt, pinned Winslow}
    (\emph{baseline}). The pipeline used throughout this section;
    the output is
    Figure~\ref{fig:l-circle-pipeline}(\subref{fig:l-ci-winslow}).
  \item \textbf{B. Sliding shape-opt, pinned Winslow}. Let
    shape-opt redistribute non-corner boundary vertices along the
    disk (with the six L-corners pinned on the circle at their
    proportional-arc positions). Derive new physical boundary
    positions from the slid disk positions via proportional arc
    length along $\partial \Omega$, and run a pinned Winslow with
    that new physical boundary as Dirichlet data.
  \item \textbf{C. Pinned shape-opt, sliding Winslow}. Keep the
    reference exactly as in (A); let Winslow's PDE solve itself
    slide the physical boundary nodes along $\partial \Omega$,
    with only the six L-corners pinned. No redistribution happens
    on the reference side at all---boundary-vertex density on the
    disk stays as proportional-arc Tutte laid it down.
  \item \textbf{D. Sliding shape-opt, sliding Winslow}. Let both
    stages slide: shape-opt adjusts the disk distribution and
    Winslow adjusts the physical distribution.
\end{itemize}

The motivation is that pinning enforces a specific boundary
density at each stage, and if those densities are suboptimal,
a sliding stage could in principle lower $\sum \eta^2$ further.
Whether the improvement on one side translates to the other is
an empirical question.

\subsubsection{Example 6: annulus (multiply connected input)}
\label{sec:tutte-ex-annulus}

Up to this point every example has been simply connected---a single
boundary loop, trivially a topological disk. \textbf{Tutte's
theorem requires this; if the input has a hole, the theorem does
not apply, and the pipeline needs a preprocessing step to turn the
input into a disk first.} The simplest such input is an annulus:
a disk with one hole, so the domain has two boundary loops (outer
and hole).

The remedy is a classical trick: choose a \emph{cut path} through
the interior of the mesh from the outer boundary to the hole
boundary, slit the mesh along it by duplicating the path vertices,
and assign each triangle on one side of the slit to use the
originals and each triangle on the other side to use the
duplicates. The resulting mesh has a single boundary loop that
traverses the original outer boundary, one side of the cut, the
original hole boundary, and the other side of the cut. Topologically
it is a disk, and Tutte applies without further modification.

\paragraph{Implementation (\texttt{cut\_to\_disk.m}).}
The helper \texttt{cut\_to\_disk(msh)} does three things. First, it
walks the directed boundary edges of the input and groups them into
oriented loops (CCW $\to$ outer, CW $\to$ hole by signed area).
Second, for each hole it computes a shortest edge-path through the
linear-vertex graph from any outer-loop vertex to any hole-loop
vertex using breadth-first search; this path is the cut. Third, it
slits the mesh along the cut by duplicating every vertex on the
path and rewriting triangles so that triangles on one side of the
cut use the original indices and triangles on the other side use
the duplicates.

\paragraph{Side classification: a trap.}
The non-obvious step is deciding, for each triangle, which side of
the cut it is on. A tempting first attempt is to do this by a BFS
on the triangle adjacency graph after marking cut edges as blocked:
start the BFS from one of the two triangles adjacent to the first
cut edge, propagate through non-cut edges, and label everything
reachable as side~1; everything else is side~2. This works on a
simply connected domain but \textbf{fails on an annulus}, because
the annulus has a non-trivial loop around the hole and the
adjacency graph minus the cut edges remains connected---BFS walks
``the long way around'' and labels the two sides inconsistently.
The correct approach is purely geometric: for each triangle,
compute its centroid and take the sign of the cross product of the
cut direction with the centroid's offset from the nearest cut
segment. This does not depend on graph connectivity at all and is
not fooled by the annular wraparound.

\paragraph{Why the 3-connectedness diagnostic fails.}
Running \texttt{is\_3\_connected\_diagnostic} on the clean input
mesh returns a $2$-vertex cut $\{v_1, v_2\}$ whose removal
disconnects the graph. In our run, $v_1$ and $v_2$ both sit on
the side walls of the rightmost tooth, at the same height
roughly halfway up the tooth
(Figure~\ref{fig:comb-cut}). \textbf{The tooth tip above them
forms a ``pendant'' subgraph attached to the rest of the mesh
only through $\{v_1, v_2\}$}, and this is exactly the obstruction
Tutte's theorem does not tolerate.

The mechanism is simple. The Delaunay sampler places $\sim\!10$
samples per unit length on the boundary, so each tooth side gets
a column of $\sim\!5$ boundary vertices. Interior points are
drawn uniformly over the comb's bounding rectangle, but the
teeth occupy a small fraction of that area, so \emph{no interior
points land inside any tooth}. With no interior vertex inside a
tooth tip, the Delaunay is forced to draw edges directly across
the tooth from left-wall samples to right-wall samples. Every
edge that crosses the tooth at the cut height passes through
$v_1$ or $v_2$, so deleting both severs every cross-tooth edge at
that height and the tip falls off.

  pendant block (tooth tip). Vertices $v_1, v_2$ (red circles,
  here labeled~$17$ and~$20$) are the only graph neighbors
  through which pendant vertices connect to the rest of the
  mesh. Removing them isolates the pink region: a $2$-vertex cut,
  i.e., graph connectivity $\le 2 < 3$.}
  \label{fig:comb-cut}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Consequences for the pipeline.}
Tutte's theorem guarantees a non-inverted straight-line embedding
\emph{only} when the linear-vertex graph is $3$-connected. Our
graph is $2$-connected, so the guarantee is voided, and the
generic failure mode for a pendant block $B$ attached by
$\{v_1, v_2\}$ is that the barycentric solve forces interior
vertices of $B$ onto the line segment $\overline{v_1 v_2}$,
collapsing or inverting the pendant triangles. This is what
Figure~\ref{fig:comb-square-pipeline} shows for the unit-square
target: Tutte collapses the pendant tooth-tips onto the short
chords between each tooth's cut vertices, the pipeline inherits
$\max\eta \approx 10^{15}$ from the collapse, shape optimization
cannot escape, and Winslow produces a nonsense output.

\paragraph{Remedy: restore $3$-connectedness by interior
anchoring.} The failure is a shortcoming of the \emph{input
meshing}, not of the domain: the comb is a perfectly ordinary
simply connected polygon, and any triangulation that resolves
each narrow tooth with at least one interior vertex is
$3$-connected. \textbf{The robust fix is to run the
$3$-connectedness diagnostic up front and, wherever it reports
a $2$-cut $\{v_1, v_2\}$, insert an interior anchor vertex inside
the pendant block that the cut isolates.} The new vertex becomes
a third independent path between the pendant block and the rest
of the graph, breaking the $2$-cut. Concretely: place the anchor
at a safe interior position near the midpoint of
$\overline{v_1 v_2}$, verify it lies inside the domain polygon,
add it to the point set, and re-triangulate with constrained
Delaunay. If the diagnostic still reports a cut, iterate. The
loop is cheap (each iteration is one Delaunay retriangulation)
and in all examples we have tested terminates in a small number
of steps.

The remedy is straightforward enough that we defer its numerical
illustration to Example~8 (Section~\ref{sec:tutte-ex-bump}),
where the same class of $3$-connectedness failure occurs in a
cleaner setting (corner-pendant rather than narrow-feature
pendant) and the anchoring fix produces a fully valid pipeline
on a recognized high-order CFD benchmark. The comb is included
here as the motivating example of what goes wrong when the fix
is omitted.

\subsection{How to Present to Persson}
\label{sec:tutte-present}


\subsubsection*{The setup}

Fortunato--Persson 2016 assumes a valid straight-sided reference
mesh is handed to you as input. 
Our motivation and question: I want to use Fortunato-Persson as is. As a black box.
Can we construct the reference a valid and well shaped reference from
the physical-domain boundary and the topology of the physical
mesh, ignoring the actual physical positions of the vertex. 

\subsubsection*{The pipeline}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Input: a physical mesh (possibly tangled), or just a
    boundary polygon + linear-vertex topology from a Delaunay.
  \item \textbf{Tutte's barycentric embedding}: fix boundary
    vertices on a convex target $P$ (unit circle or unit square);
    interior vertices are the Laplacian solution. Straight-line
    embedding with no inverted elements, guaranteed by Tutte (1963).
  \item \textbf{Shape optimization} on the straight-sided Tutte
    output: minimize $\sum_K \eta_K^2$ over interior-vertex
    positions (boundary pinned), using BFGS with the equilateral
    triangle as the ideal.
  \item \textbf{High-order promotion} via \texttt{nodealloc}.
  \item \textbf{Winslow hand-off}: run the original
    Fortunato--Persson 2016 solver on the Step~1 input, using the
    result of Step~4 as the reference---our constructed
    well-shaped reference mesh.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection*{Difficulty progression}

The examples are ordered to walk through a difficulty ladder.
Each step introduces one new piece of non-triviality while
keeping the pipeline identical; the point is to show what
changes as we raise the geometric difficulty.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Example~1 (w2 grid):} sanity check, nothing
    non-trivial. Tutte recovers the input grid; shape-opt is a
    no-op.
  \item \textbf{Example~2 (unit square, unstructured):} Tutte
    solve is non-trivial (unstructured topology), Winslow is
    trivial (physical and reference are the same square).
  \item \textbf{Example~3 (stadium):} Tutte solve non-trivial
    \emph{and} Winslow non-trivial. Everything still works
    cleanly because the physical is convex and smooth.
  \item \textbf{Example~4 (starfish):} smooth but now
    non-convex. The per-arm wedge-compression mechanism appears
    on the disk reference; Winslow's bounded-distortion map
    transports it back into a valid physical mesh.
  \item \textbf{Example~5 (L-shape):} sharp concave corners.
    Pipeline still works in pinned-boundary mode but the
    concave-corner geometry introduces additional caveats.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Walking through the examples}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Example~1 (Section~\ref{sec:tutte-ex-w2}): regular
    grid from \texttt{w2.m}.} Sanity check. The tangled input's
    underlying topology is a regular $11 \times 11$ grid of
    right-isosceles triangles; Tutte recovers exactly that grid
    from the topology alone, and shape-opt starts at a stationary
    point because the uniform grid is already a local minimum of
    $\sum_K \eta_K^2$. The Winslow hand-off then reproduces the
    canonical \texttt{w2.m} output.

  \item \textbf{Example~2 (Section~\ref{sec:tutte-ex-unstructured}):
    unstructured Delaunay on the unit square.} Here the physical
    domain and the Tutte target are both the unit square, so the
    Winslow step is trivial: given the constructed reference
    mesh, Winslow recovers it unchanged (identity map). The
    interesting part is the \emph{Tutte solve and shape-opt}.
    Unlike Example~1 the topology is unstructured (random
    interior points, varying valences), so Tutte's Laplacian
    system is genuinely non-trivial and shape optimization has
    real work to do to turn the Laplacian embedding into a
    well-shaped triangulation. This example validates the Tutte
    $+$ shape-opt sub-pipeline as a reference-mesh generator in
    isolation from Winslow.

  \item \textbf{Example~3 (Section~\ref{sec:tutte-ex-stadium}):
    convex stadium.} The first case where every stage is doing
    non-trivial work \emph{and} produces a clean output. Aspect
    ratio $3\!:\!1$ gives Tutte a substantive mapping problem.
    Two sliver clumps appear on the Tutte disk---one per
    semicircular end-cap of the physical---establishing
    ``clumps on the reference are diagnostics of physical
    boundary density regions'' as a general framing.
    Winslow on both paths (with and without shape-opt)
    converges quickly and the two outputs are visually
    indistinguishable.

  \item \textbf{Example~4 (Section~\ref{sec:tutte-ex-starfish}):
    smooth non-convex starfish.} The first case where every
    stage is doing non-trivial work.
    \emph{Key difficulty}: each arm has a perimeter share
    comparable to the bays' but occupies roughly $1/5$ of the
    area, so Tutte's proportional-arc mapping compresses each
    arm onto a thin angular wedge of the disk, producing an
    inward fan of slivers
    (Fig.~\ref{fig:star-pipeline}\subref{fig:star-tutte}).
    Shape-opt cannot un-compress those wedges because the
    boundary is pinned; it only polishes interior positions.
    \emph{How it still works}: Winslow's reference-to-physical
    map has bounded distortion on smooth boundaries, and the
    local Jacobian---radially-stretching, tangentially-gentle in
    an arm---composes with the slivered reference triangles to
    produce moderate-aspect-ratio triangles on the physical arms
    (Fig.~\ref{fig:star-pipeline}\subref{fig:star-winslow}).
    The pipeline outputs a clean physical mesh despite the
    unflattering intermediate geometry.
    \emph{Cost of shape-opt}: the BFGS optimizer runs
    $\sim 250$ iterations with analytic gradients, and $\sum
    \eta^2$ drops by roughly half on the reference. By
    $\eta$-based metrics the shape-opt pipeline wins (mean
    $\eta^2$ $3.51$ vs $6.84$; max $\eta$ $4.70$ vs $10.46$).
    But the ablation in
    Fig.~\ref{fig:star-pipeline}\subref{fig:star-winslow-from-tutte}
    shows the no-shape-opt Winslow output is visually comparable
    and can even look more uniform in element size---because
    $\eta$ is scale-invariant, shape-opt has no preference for
    equally-sized elements, only for equally-\emph{shaped} ones.
    See Section~\ref{sec:tutte-ex-starfish} for the detailed
    discussion and implications for size-aware objectives.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{What works cleanly}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Simply connected smooth non-convex domains (the
    starfish).} The pipeline produces dramatically cleaner
    physical meshes than the raw Delaunay input. The Winslow
    step transports the reference mesh back to the physical
    domain smoothly, and the per-arm sliver fans on the disk
    unwind into decent radial fans on the arms. The remaining
    artefact---a boundary-adjacent sliver ring on the disk, one
    sliver fan per physical arm---is structural to the
    convex-target choice and motivates the sliding / flipping
    extensions.
  \item \textbf{Simply connected with narrow pointy appendages}
    (hand, star, dolphin fluke). Tutte's theorem requires the
    linear-vertex graph to be $3$-connected; pointy convex corners
    produce degree-$2$ boundary vertices that violate this. Fix:
    detect degree-$2$ boundary vertices, plant an interior anchor
    nudged inward from the boundary. Systematic detection,
    closed-form fix, works without manual tuning.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Extensions we explored}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Boundary sliding in shape optimization.} Let
    boundary vertices slide tangentially along $\partial P$ during
    the optimization (angles for circle, arc-lengths for polygon).
    Produces substantially better reference-mesh quality: on the
    L-shape, $\sum \eta^2$ drops by $\sim 45\%$ relative to the
    pinned-boundary version.
  \item \textbf{Proportional-arc Tutte + corner pinning + derive.}
    When the reference boundary slides, the Winslow hand-off
    loses reference--physical correspondence and Picard diverges.
    Fix: (i) map the input's boundary onto $\partial P$ by
    proportional arc length so domain corners land at
    topologically-correct places; (ii) pin those corners during
    shape optimization so they don't drift; (iii) at hand-off
    time, \emph{derive} the physical-boundary Dirichlet data
    from the slid reference via proportional-arc mapping rather
    than reusing the input's sampling. The derive step is the
    component that makes Winslow converge; the others ensure the
    derived physical domain retains its correct geometry.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Where it breaks}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Simply connected with sharp concave corners (L-shape).}
    The sliding extension improves reference quality, but the
    proportional-arc map transports boundary clustering from the
    reference onto the physical, and clustered vertices at a
    physical concave corner produce sliver outliers. Net effect
    on the L-shape: sliding improves the reference mesh
    ($\sum \eta^2$ down), but the final Winslow output is
    \emph{worse} than the pinned-boundary pipeline at the worst
    element (max $\eta$ jumps from $6.7$ to $16.5$). Pinned
    Winslow stays the primary pipeline for this class of domain.
  \item \textbf{Multiply connected domains.} Tutte requires a
    topological disk. A hole-slitting preprocess (\texttt{cut\_to\_disk})
    is the classical remedy; we implemented and removed it.
    Currently out of scope.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Open directions}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Edge flipping + smoothing alternation.} Lawson's
    flipping algorithm terminates at the Delaunay of the current
    vertex positions; alternating it with shape optimization is
    known to drive $\sum \eta^2$ close to a local minimum that is
    ``as good as it gets'' for the fixed vertex set on the
    reference. Cheap to add, likely big win on the reference,
    unclear how much propagates to the physical.
  \item \textbf{Angle-gap barrier in sliding.} Soft penalty on
    clustering in the slide step so that the beneficial
    redistribution happens without the pathological clustering at
    concave-corner regions on the physical.
  \item \textbf{Physical-aware shape-opt objective.} Instead of
    minimizing $\sum \eta^2$ on the reference, minimize a proxy
    for the post-Winslow mesh quality (e.g., $\sum \eta^2$
    evaluated on the derived physical boundary). Addresses the
    reference-vs-physical mismatch directly rather than working
    around it.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Honest framing of the contribution}

We are extending empirical FP-2016 to the case of
reference-mesh-from-scratch. We inherit the same
``works-in-practice-on-our-test-cases'' standard as the 2016 paper;
we do not prove convergence or optimality. The scope is simply
connected 2D domains, with specific remedies for narrow
appendages (anchoring) and curved targets (\texttt{msh.fd}
hookup). Boundary sliding is a further extension that is an
unalloyed improvement for convex or smooth physical domains, and
a more nuanced tradeoff for sharp-cornered non-convex ones.
